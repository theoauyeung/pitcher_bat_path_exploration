# Results

## 5.1 Population-Level Disruption Metrics

Applied to 763,833 competitive swings recorded across the 2023–2025 MLB seasons, the causal mediation pipeline produced per-swing estimates of disruption tax, distortion tax, and selection tax for each of the 1,214 pitchers with at least 50 qualifying swings. Across the full population, the mean disruption tax was −0.002 runs per swing, reflecting a slight but consistent net pitcher advantage attributable to post-commit pitch behavior. Decomposing this figure revealed that the two components work in structurally opposite directions at the population level: mean distortion tax was +0.001 runs per swing — a marginal average benefit to batters from post-commit spatial displacement — while mean selection tax was −0.003 runs per swing, indicating that batters' own swing-shape deviations from their intentions cost them more than post-commit movement gained them. The slight average gain from spatial displacement is not evidence that movement favors batters broadly; rather, it reflects that across all pitch types and counts, ball flight tends to arrive near expected locations on average, and the metric captures the variance around that mean — specifically, the minority of pitches with substantial post-commit movement that systematically displaces the ball to a worse crossing point for the batter. At the per-swing level, 61.1% of swings showed a distortion share exceeding 50%, meaning that for a majority of disrupted swings, post-commit movement accounted for more than half of the total run-value cost to the batter. The mean miss distortion tax was −0.003 runs per swing, consistent with post-commit movement causing a measurable increase in bat-to-ball separation beyond what would have occurred with a straight trajectory.

The decision cost measure confirmed that batters self-select their swings rationally: the population mean was −0.083 runs per swing, meaning that on the swings batters chose to take, swinging was almost universally the correct decision. In 16.2% of swings, the decision cost was positive — taking the pitch would have been the superior outcome given the projected plate location and count state — and for these swings the adjusted disruption tax properly shifts the comparison baseline from the intended-swing xRV to the take value, yielding a more conservative measure of the pitcher's causal burden on the batter. An important structural property of the adjusted metric is that at 0-2 and 1-2 counts, the run-value framework assigns nearly equal cost to a strikeout via whiff and a strikeout via called strike (both end the plate appearance with equivalent run expectancy change), meaning the adjusted measure rarely penalizes 2-strike swing decisions at borderline locations. Readers should interpret the adjusted disruption tax at these counts as reflecting distortion burden conditional on a rational protect-the-plate stance, not as endorsing every 2-strike chase as the correct decision.

## 5.2 Reliability as Evidence of Repeatable Pitcher Skill

If distortion tax is to be useful as a pitcher evaluation tool, it must be repeatable — that is, pitchers who generate high distortion tax in one sample should continue to do so in independent samples. We assessed reliability through two approaches: (1) Spearman-Brown corrected split-half correlation averaged over 100 random splits, and (2) year-over-year Pearson correlation and ICC(2,1) between consecutive-season pitcher means (minimum 50 swings per season per pitcher). The results, shown in **Figure `08_reliability.png`**, confirm that distortion tax is the most reliable of the four metrics. It exhibited a split-half Spearman-Brown correlation of 0.893 — approaching test-retest reliabilities associated with established pitching metrics — and ICC(2,1) of 0.806, reflecting high absolute agreement between the two halves beyond what chance would produce. Year-over-year correlation was 0.659 in both the 2023–2024 and 2024–2025 transitions, with matching ICC values of 0.659 and 0.655 respectively. The close alignment between Pearson r and ICC in the year-over-year analysis indicates that the metric is not only tracking the correct rank order across pitchers but also producing estimates with low systematic bias across seasons.

Disruption tax — the composite measure prior to the distortion/selection decomposition — showed somewhat lower but still strong reliability (split-half r = 0.842, ICC = 0.727; YoY r ≈ 0.608). Selection tax was the least reliable of the components (split-half r = 0.715, YoY r = 0.39–0.49), consistent with the interpretation that a batter's swing-shape deviation is partly idiosyncratic and influenced by factors external to the pitcher (pitch recognition, count, fatigue), whereas the portion attributable to movement is the repeatable, pitcher-controlled signal. The adjusted disruption tax, which incorporates the decision cost correction, showed intermediate reliability (split-half r = 0.749, YoY r = 0.50–0.53), with the reduction relative to the unadjusted metric reflecting additional variance introduced by count-state swing decisions that cannot be entirely attributed to the pitcher.

The pattern of reliabilities across components carries a direct methodological implication: distortion tax is the appropriate primary metric for pitcher evaluation precisely because it isolates the pitcher-controlled component of disruption. Selection tax, while estimable, should be interpreted with greater caution given its lower year-to-year stability.

## 5.3 Kinematic Case Illustrations

To make the metric interpretable beyond aggregate leaderboards, we constructed broadcast-style annotation cards for four illustrative pitches, pairing game footage with a quantitative disruption analysis panel. The four cases were chosen to span the range of disruption sources — from nearly pure distortion through mixed attribution to nearly pure selection — and to illustrate how the metric handles qualitatively different types of disruption (Figures `yamamoto_bernabel_annotation.png`, `leiter_ramirez_annotation.png`, `helsley_mullins_annotation.png`, `sale_harper_annotation.png`).

**Case 1: Yoshinobu Yamamoto vs. José Bernabéi, Curveball, 2-0 Count.** Yamamoto's curveball at 76.1 mph generated −14.2 inches of induced vertical drop (pfx_z) and −5.7 inches of post-commit vertical displacement from projected to actual plate crossing. The ball projected to cross at 27.0 inches from the ground — already below the strike zone — but further dropped to 21.3 inches by the actual crossing, a 5.7-inch shortfall that Bernabéi's visual system had no opportunity to correct for after commitment. His bat-speed of 68.6 mph and a realized vertical attack angle of +23.7° indicated he was reaching hard downward for the ball, but with a ball-bat miss of only 3.7 inches despite the dramatic movement, his swing shape tracked the trajectory reasonably closely. The disruption tax of −0.048 runs and distortion share of 99.7% confirm that virtually all of the run-value cost on this pitch was attributable to Yamamoto's post-commit movement, not to Bernabéi's swing decision. This pitch represents the prototypical high-distortion outcome: a pitcher's signature movement forces the ball off its projected trajectory in a way the batter cannot mechanically compensate for, and the causal attribution clearly separates the pitcher's contribution.

**Case 2: Mark Leiter Jr. vs. Harold Ramírez, Curveball, 1-0 Count.** Leiter's curveball, nearly identical in movement profile to the Yamamoto pitch (73.4 mph, −17.9 inches pfx_z, −6.0 inches post-commit drop), produced a strikingly different attribution outcome. The disruption tax on this pitch was −0.005 runs — minimal — and the distortion share was 0.0%, with a selection tax of −0.012 runs absorbing the angular component. Despite the pitch dropping 6.0 inches after commit, Ramírez's swing-shape deviation was driven primarily by his own unforced error in swing selection rather than being mechanically caused by the pitch movement. The mediator model estimated that the movement-caused component of his angular deviation was small relative to the total deviation he exhibited, indicating that he largely adjusted his swing plane of his own volition, not because the movement forced him to. This comparison against the Yamamoto case illustrates the core identification challenge the framework addresses: equal post-commit movement does not imply equal distortion tax, because the batter's angular response may not be mechanically traceable to that movement. The mediator models are essential to this distinction — without the causal attribution step, both pitches would appear equally disruptive based on movement alone.

**Case 3: Ryan Helsley vs. Cedric Mullins, Four-Seam Fastball, 1-2 Count.** At 100.0 mph with +18.2 inches of induced vertical rise (pfx_z), Helsley's four-seam fastball at a 1-2 count provides a contrasting case where the post-commit disruption is almost entirely self-selected. The ball crossed the plate at 47.9 inches (projected: 49.2 inches, a mere 1.4-inch post-commit vertical deviation), but both locations were approximately 9 inches above Mullins' strike zone top of 40.3 inches. Mullins swung anyway, resulting in a 4.4-inch ball-bat miss and a +23.7° attack angle on a pitch well out of the zone. The distortion share of 5.0% indicates that essentially all of the run-value disruption was self-selected — Mullins chose to swing at a pitch he had projected above the zone, with negligible additional displacement after commit. The positive adjusted disruption tax (+0.028 runs) reflects that at a 1-2 count, the run-value difference between swinging and missing (strikeout) and taking a ball (moving to 2-2) is modest, such that the swing was not dramatically suboptimal from a pure run-expectancy perspective even though it was objectively a bad pitch to chase. This case demonstrates both the appropriate application of the adjusted metric and its inherent limitation at 2-strike counts noted in Section 5.1. The broader value for pitcher evaluation is instructive: Helsley's velocity and ride create a pitch profile that is effective through perceptual deception rather than post-commit mechanical disruption — the batter makes a bad decision based on the pitch's approach, not because movement after commit displaced the ball beyond his adjustment capacity.

**Case 4: Chris Sale vs. Bryce Harper, Slider, 0-1 Count.** Sale's 77.7 mph slider generated 4.5 inches of post-commit vertical drop and a horizontal deviation of −0.9 inches, resulting in a plate crossing 17.3 inches below the batter's strike zone top. The ball-bat miss of 40.4 inches — among the largest recorded in the dataset — reflects a swing that missed the pitch by over three feet, with Harper's realized attack angle of +24.7° representing a steep upward path toward a pitch that was cutting hard downward and away. The distortion share of 56.7% places this pitch in the mixed-attribution category: the 4.5-inch post-commit displacement and resulting swing-angle forcing accounted for a majority of the cost, with the remainder (selection tax = −0.009 runs) attributable to Harper's own swing shape. Importantly, the decision cost of +0.057 runs — positive, meaning taking this pitch would have been the marginally superior action — shifts the baseline for the adjusted disruption tax from Harper's intended swing value to his take value, yielding an adjusted disruption tax of −0.079 runs versus the raw disruption tax of −0.021 runs. This illustrates a key property of the adjusted metric: when a batter swings at a pitch they should have taken and the pitch also distorts their swing mechanically, the adjusted measure captures the full compounded cost — the distortion burden plus the cost of the swing decision itself — rather than attributing it all to the pitcher's movement. From Sale's perspective, his slider generated genuine mechanical disruption (distortion tax = −0.012) while also inducing a poor swing decision, a combination that makes the pitch doubly effective in run-value terms.

## 5.4 Pitcher Leaderboard and Empirical-Bayes Estimation

Across the 981 pitcher-seasons in the 2023–2025 sample (minimum 200 swings per season), the distribution of pitcher-level distortion tax showed substantial spread, with empirical-Bayes (EB) shrunk values ranging from −0.015 to +0.013 runs per swing in the 2024 season. Because individual-season means carry noise proportional to sample size, EB shrinkage toward the grand mean was applied using the James-Stein formula, weighting each pitcher's observed mean by the reliability coefficient n/(n + within-variance/between-variance). The resulting shrunk estimates are displayed in **Figure `distortion_tax_leaderboard_2024.png`** for the top and bottom 20 pitchers in 2024.

The pitchers with the most pitcher-advantageous distortion tax in 2024 — Andrew Chafin (−0.015 runs/swing), Elvis Peguero (−0.013), Framber Valdez (−0.012), Zac Gallen (−0.012), and Tanner Houck (−0.012) — represent a diverse set of profiles united by late-breaking movement with large post-commit displacement. Valdez's sinker consistently drops 6–8 inches below projected trajectories after batter commitment, forcing downward swing-plane adjustments that neither reach the ball nor generate useful contact. Gallen's slider and changeup generate similar angular forcing on the vertical axis. Houck's slider is known for its late tilt. The consistency of sinker/breaking-ball profiles at the top of the distortion leaderboard aligns with the physical driver analysis described in Section 5.6.

At the opposite end of the distribution, pitchers including Matt Waldron (+0.012 runs/swing) and Tyler Rogers (+0.012) show the most batter-favorable distortion profiles. Both are submarine-delivery pitchers whose post-commit movement, while unusual in absolute terms, tends to arrive slightly closer to the strike zone than batters' pre-commit projections — meaning the ball materializes in a more hittable position than anticipated. This finding has an important interpretive implication: a positive distortion tax does not mean the pitcher is ineffective. Submarine pitchers are effective through visual deception that occurs before the commit point, not through post-commit movement that displaces the ball beyond the batter's adjustment window. Distortion tax specifically measures the latter, and two pitchers can have identical effectiveness ratings while differing substantially in their distortion profiles depending on where in the pitch flight their deceptive leverage is concentrated.

## 5.5 Angular Axis Fingerprint

We normalized each pitcher's movement-caused angular disruption across the three axes — vertical attack angle (VAA), horizontal attack angle (HAA), and swing path tilt — to determine which axis post-commit movement most consistently forces. Tilt was dominant in 926 of 981 pitcher-seasons (94.4%); HAA dominated the remaining 55 (5.6%); VAA was never the primary axis. Mean shares were tilt 41.7%, HAA 40.1%, VAA 18.2% — HAA carries nearly equal population-average weight despite rarely being dominant, because it is nearly constant across pitchers (σ = 0.001) while tilt and VAA trade off inversely.

The 55 HAA-dominant pitcher-seasons were exclusively left-handed pitchers. **Figure `axis_fingerprint.png`** shows this structure: the left panel scatters VAA_share against tilt margin (tilt_share − HAA_share) by pitcher-season and handedness — the near-perfect negative linear relationship is a consequence of HAA being nearly fixed while tilt and VAA compete for the remainder. LHP exclusively fall below the y = 0 HAA-dominance boundary. For pitch design, tilt-disrupting profiles (late horizontal cut or tilt-changing movement) generate more angular disruption per unit of post-commit displacement than designs optimizing for pure vertical drop.

## 5.6 Physical Drivers of Distortion Tax

We fit an OLS regression at the pitcher-pitch-type level (n ≥ 100 swings per pitcher-pitch-type, HC1 SE) predicting mean distortion tax from movement and delivery parameters. R² = 0.552. Results are shown in **Figure `physical_drivers_ols.png`**.

Vertical spin movement was the dominant predictor (β = +0.056, p < 0.01): more negative induced vertical break (sinkers, curveballs, changeups) produces more pitcher-advantageous distortion tax; positive rise (four-seam) produces batter-favorable distortion. Arm-side horizontal movement was significant (β = +0.005, p < 0.001), with more arm-side cut weakly associated with less batter-harmful distortion. Release extension was negative (β = −0.002, p = 0.009) — closer release compresses the post-commit adjustment window. Release speed was positive (β = +0.001, p < 0.001). Arm angle (β = −0.0001, p < 0.001) and vertical gyro spin (β = −0.004, p = 0.012) were both significant; horizontal gyro spin was not (p = 0.81).

For pitch design, induced vertical break is the primary lever: adding sinker depth or curveball drop should directly increase distortion tax, with extension gains compounding the effect.

## 5.7 Incremental Predictive Validity

We assessed incremental validity via two-stage regression (2024→2025): baseline model uses current-season outcome alone; full model adds current-season distortion tax. Two outcomes: next-season ball-bat miss on whiffs and next-season xRV per swing.

Ball-bat miss baseline R² = 0.553 (n = 299). Adding distortion tax: full R² = 0.555, ΔR² = 0.002, β = 8.717, p = 0.236. xRV baseline R² = 0.329 (n = 328). Adding distortion tax: full R² = 0.334, ΔR² = 0.004, β = −0.116, p = 0.143. Neither effect was significant.

The non-significant incremental effects reflect the strong autoregressive structure of both outcomes — distortion tax is correlated with ball-bat miss and xRV because they are downstream of it, leaving limited residual variance to explain. Distortion tax's primary value is mechanistic characterization, not short-window forecasting.

## 5.8 Application and Implications for Pitcher Evaluation

Taken together, the results establish distortion tax as a reliable, physically interpretable, and mechanistically grounded measure of a pitcher's capacity to generate post-commit movement-induced disruption. Its value to practitioners lies at several levels.

At the most immediate level, distortion tax decomposes a pitch outcome into the pitcher's causal contribution and the batter's own swing-shape error, resolving an identification problem that aggregate statistics cannot address. A pitcher with a 30% whiff rate may achieve that outcome through distortion (movement forces misses the batter cannot adjust to), through deception (the batter makes an early commitment error before movement begins), or through selection (the batter swings at pitches where swinging was already wrong). Distortion tax measures the first channel specifically, enabling evaluation questions like: "Has this pitcher's sinker movement gotten more disruptive this season, or has his whiff rate risen because opponents are making worse swing decisions?" The axis fingerprint additionally clarifies whether a pitcher's disruption is primarily angular (tilt/HAA) or spatial (projected location displacement), which has direct implications for what type of pitch design modification would increase disruption.

For player development and pitch design, the physical driver results translate directly into pitch design targets. A pitcher redesigning a changeup to add more pfx_z depth — through grip changes that reduce spin efficiency or seam orientation — should expect an increase in distortion tax on that pitch type, all else equal. The release extension result similarly suggests that mechanics adjustments that increase effective extension (release point closer to the plate) may marginally amplify distortion by compressing the batter's post-commit adjustment window.

For scouting and acquisition, the year-over-year reliability of 0.659 indicates that distortion tax carries meaningful signal about a pitcher's future capability. A pitcher with consistently high distortion tax across multiple seasons has demonstrated the ability to create movement that mechanically disrupts batting mechanics, not merely to benefit from favorable batter decisions. Conversely, a pitcher with positive distortion tax (movement that helps batters) but high whiff rates may be achieving results through perceptual deception that is more fragile — as hitters adjust or exposure increases within a season, the deception advantage may erode while the movement profile is already batter-favorable.

Finally, the case illustrations underscore that the metric's interpretive power is amplified when combined with qualitative pitch-by-pitch analysis. A broadcast card showing 5.7 inches of post-commit vertical drop, 99.7% distortion attribution, and a bat missing the ball by 3.7 inches communicates in a single frame what traditional pitch metrics require multiple tables to approximate: the exact causal chain from release mechanics to swing failure, priced in the currency that determines game outcomes.
